### Why Centralisation is the Death of Empathy

The current state of British politics is routinely blamed on individual incompetence, for example, the rapid and dramatic collapse in public support for Keir Starmer. However, tracking record-low approval ratings misses the deeper structural crisis. The visceral public backlash isn't merely a failure of political public relations; it is the inevitable byproduct of a systemic egoism.

When a country faces decades of compounding systemic decay across public services, infrastructure, health, and education, the standard Westminster response is to reach for the "centralisation warhorse". The modern regime genuinely operates on the assumption that a deeply fragmented, broken system can be salvaged through top-down command-and-control. The aggressive moves to dismantle regional autonomy, such as bringing NHS management under direct ministerial oversight or merging smaller local police forces into larger regional bodies, are framed as rescue missions, whereas in reality, they are ideological blunders.

This central monopoly is mathematically anchored by the First-Past-The-Post electoral system, which manufactures artificial majorities out of minor mandates. By allowing a political faction to seize total, unchecked legislative control with a mere minority of the popular vote, First-Past-The-Post removes any structural requirement for consensus or cooperation. It turns Westminster into a winner-takes-all dictatorship of the centre, ensuring that power remains permanently consolidated at the top while local anomalies and regional voices are ironed out.
